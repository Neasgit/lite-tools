02 · References · Version 1.0

Title & Thumbnail.

Treat the title and the thumbnail as one paired asset. The title creates the question. The thumbnail shows the evidence.

Patterns 7 Updated May 2026 Companion Brand Reference ·08·09
01 · The pair principle

A title and a thumbnail do two different jobs. When they repeat each other the listing feels weaker · the viewer reads the same thing twice. When they complement each other, the title raises a question and the thumbnail answers with a piece of evidence.

TITLE

Creates the question.

Names the specific document, footage, call, statement, officer, record, or interview. Specific. Sourced. Restrained.

THUMBNAIL

Shows the evidence.

One image, one phrase, one number · whatever the title points at. Never a copy of the title.

03 · Title rules

Specific. Sourced. Restrained.

? Do

  • Use one concrete noun: report, footage, call, timeline, statement, officer, record, interview.
  • Keep the title specific to this case · not a category headline.
  • Let the title create the question. The thumbnail will show the evidence.
  • Use uncertainty carefully: allegedly, appears to, according to.
  • Front-load the most distinctive word · viewers scan, they don't read.

? Avoid

  • "YOU WON'T BELIEVE" · reaction-channel grammar.
  • All caps · looks like every other bodycam channel.
  • Generic category labels: "Crazy Bodycam Footage" tells you nothing.
  • Repeating the thumbnail text · wasted real estate.
  • Overstating certainty: "PROVES he lied." Use appears to contradict.
