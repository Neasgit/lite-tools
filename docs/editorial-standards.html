02 · References · Version 1.1

Editorial Standards.

Evidence before narrative. The rules every claim must pass before a script locks, before a thumbnail ships, before a video publishes.

Version 1.1 Updated 2026-05-21 Source Brand Review & Action Plan
01 · Principle

Evidence-led, not conclusion-led.

Every claim in a script, thumbnail, title, or description must be supported by footage, court records, police statements, official documents, or clearly attributed reporting. If a claim cannot be sourced, it gets rewritten or removed · not softened.

These standards exist so the channel stays trustworthy as it grows. Once trust is lost it does not come back. Bake the rules into the pipeline and you stop having to make the same judgement call every week.

02 · The Rules

9 rules · enforced at script and at review

01
Every claim must be supported by footage, court records, police statements, official documents, or clearly attributed reporting.
02
Do not imply guilt beyond what the footage or records support.
03
Do not use gore, grief, or distress as the hook.
04
Do not include graphic moments unless they materially change the viewer's understanding.
05
Use careful attribution · see ·03.
06
Correct errors visibly. A pinned comment, a card, or an on-screen note · not a quiet edit.
07
Do not frame real people as characters for entertainment.
08
Do not overstate certainty where the record is unclear.
09
If a claim cannot be sourced · rewrite it or remove it. There is no third option.
03 · Attribution Language

The phrases that protect the channel and the viewer. Exact wording matters.

? Use

  • allegedlyFor unresolved or contested claims; never for settled facts.
  • police sayWhen the source is a department statement or report.
  • court records stateFor anything from filings, transcripts, or rulings.
  • the footage appears to showWhen interpretation is involved · leaves room for the viewer.
  • according to the reportFor specific documents; cite the report by name when possible.
  • reportedlyFor named-source reporting that isn't yet in the public record.

? Avoid

  • obviously guiltyConclusion-led. Replace with evidence.
  • it's clear thatIf it's clear, the footage will show it. Cut the framing.
  • caught red-handedEditorial. Loaded. Doesn't survive a defamation lawyer.
  • cold-blooded killerFrames a person, not their actions.
  • in his sick mindSpeculation about state of mind without record.
  • monster, animal, predatorCharacter labels. Reaction-channel grammar.
04 · Audio Caption Style

For audio-only segments (911 calls, dispatch radio, court audio without on-screen video) the visualizer carries the tone. Locked 2026-05-20.

Thin line over thick bar.

LITE uses the thin-line waveform for any audio-only segment. The thick-bar and circular waveform treatments read too kinetic for the documentary register · they belong to reaction and true-crime-entertainment grammar, not the public-record framing this channel works in.

Spec lives on the Brand Reference page (§12 Captions). The rule lives here because it is an editorial decision about register, not just a visual one.

? Use

  • thin-line waveform911 calls, dispatch radio, off-camera court audio · matches documentary pace.

? Avoid

  • thick-bar visualizerReads as reaction-channel grammar · too kinetic.
  • circular waveformDecorative · pulls focus from the speaker label and caption text.
