07

Brand Voice

Documentary register — evidence-first, not sensationalist

Tone

Measured, serious, authoritative. The footage speaks — you provide context, not hype. Treat every case like a documentary, not a reaction video.

"The bodycam shows what happened. Here's what it means."

What it is

Evidence-first. Investigative. Specific. You respect the viewer enough to let them form opinions. You give them the material, not just the conclusion.

"At 4:12, the officer's account contradicts the footage."

What it isn't

Shock-first. Sensationalist. Vague or over-dramatic. No "YOU WON'T BELIEVE THIS." No gore as content. No commentary that treats real events as entertainment.

Not: "This is the CRAZIEST bodycam you'll EVER see!!"
DO
JCS Criminal Psychology
Almost no creator on camera. Cold and clinical. Minimal text. The editing carries the case. JCS built the most respected channel in the genre by trusting the footage.
DON'T
Mid-tier bodycam channels
Police-tape yellow, Impact font, shock-face thumbnail. It works at small scale. In the feed it looks like a hundred other channels. No viewer loyalty follows.

Lower Thirds — Yellow vs White

One rule · Always consistent · Never mix within the same scene
Amber text — first appearance
OFFICER HAYES
First time this person appears on screen in this video. Amber draws the eye — signals this is someone new the viewer needs to register.
White text — repeat appearance
OFFICER HAYES
Second or later appearance in the same video. White is quieter — the viewer already knows who this is. Don't pull focus unnecessarily.
Edge case:If a person reappears after a significant time gap (e.g. a different day of footage), treat it as a first appearance and use amber again.

Corner Tag — Specification

POV identifier — whose bodycam is the viewer watching through

The corner tag identifies whose POV the viewer is currently watching. When the cut shows Officer 1's bodycam, the tag reads OFFICER 1. When the edit switches to Officer 2's bodycam, the tag becomes OFFICER 2. It's a who-is-recording-this label, not a who-is-on-screen label. The viewer can always glance at the corner and know which officer's eyes they're watching through.

Preferred labels in order of clarity: if the officer's last name is in the public record, use OFFICER MARTINEZ. If a unit/badge number is in the record, use UNIT 4-T-12. Fall back to generic numbering (OFFICER 1, OFFICER 2…) only when nothing identifying is publicly available, or when the case calls for anonymisation.

Naming convention
OFFICER MARTINEZ, OFFICER REYES… — preferred when last names are in the public record.
UNIT 4-T-12, BADGE 1247… — preferred when unit/badge numbers are in the record.
OFFICER 1, OFFICER 2, OFFICER 3… — fallback when no identifier is public or the case warrants anonymising.
DASHCAM — for in-car footage.
CCTV, SURVEILLANCE — for fixed cameras.
INTERVIEW — for sit-down or court interviews. Optional — can be left off if the context is obvious.
The POV principle
The tag tracks the source of the footage, not the people in it. If three officers are in frame on Officer 1's bodycam, the tag still reads OFFICER 1 POV — the audience knows whose vantage point they're seeing. The moment the edit cuts to Officer 2's bodycam, the tag updates.
Numbering rules
Numbers are assigned in order of first bodycam appearance in the video — whoever's bodycam plays first is Officer 1.
Once assigned, numbers stay locked for the entire video. Officer 1 is always Officer 1, even if they don't reappear for ten minutes.
Don't renumber across episodes — if a case has multiple parts, the numbering carries between parts so the audience can keep track.
When in doubt, prefer named tags (OFFICER MARTINEZ) over numbered tags — numbers are the fallback, not the default.

Footage Treatment

Consistent grade across every video — clinical, not punchy
Apply the same grade to every bodycam and dashcam clip. Raw footage varies wildly in colour temperature, exposure, and saturation — a consistent grade ties it into a single channel. Target JCS Criminal Psychology's register: clinical, slightly cool, grey. The footage should look like evidence.
Where this happens in the pipeline: all colour values below target DaVinci Resolve's Color Wheels (Resolve is your grading tool of choice). Premiere and CapCut do no colour work — they handle assembly, captions and finishing only.
Saturation
–25 to –40%
Partial desaturation — not B&W, not raw colour. Uniform across all clips in the same video. Pick one value per project and stick to it.
Colour Temp
–150 to –200K
Shift slightly cool in Resolve's Color Wheels. Counteracts the warm orange white-balance most bodycams default to.
Lift / Gain
+0.02 / –0.07
Lift shadows slightly (+0.02–0.04) to avoid crushing black body armour. Roll off highlights (–0.05–0.10) to prevent blown windows and sky.
DO
Apply the same node stack to every clip — save it as a Resolve still or power grade
Grade thumbnails to match — what the viewer sees in the feed should match what's in the video
Slightly lift shadows on night footage — bodycams crush dark scenes badly without it
DON'T
Orange-teal grade — the most overused colour treatment on the internet
Heavy vignette — makes documentary footage look like a music video
Film grain unless very subtle (under 5%) — adds noise without adding anything
Full desaturation (B&W) — removes the realism that makes bodycam footage impactful
