Title & Thumbnail System

Treat the title and the thumbnail as one paired asset. Title creates the question. Thumbnail shows the evidence.

Version 1.0 · May 2026
Source: Brand Review & Action Plan
01

The pair principle

A title and a thumbnail do two different jobs

The title and the thumbnail are one asset, not two. They have to be designed together. When they repeat each other the listing feels weaker — the viewer reads the same thing twice. When they complement each other, the title raises a question and the thumbnail answers it with a piece of evidence.

Title
Creates the question. Names the specific document, footage, call, statement, officer, record, or interview.
Thumbnail
Shows the evidence. One image, one phrase, one number — whatever the title points at.

03

Title rules

Specific, sourced, restrained
Do
Use one concrete noun: report, footage, call, timeline, statement, officer, record, interview.
Keep the title specific to this case — not a category headline.
Let the title create the question. The thumbnail will show the evidence.
Use uncertainty carefully when needed: appears to, police say, records state.
Sentence case, not title case. Reads like an editorial line, not a headline shouter.
Don't
No all-caps titles.
No INSANE, CRAZY, SHOCKING, or YOU WON'T BELIEVE.
No guilt-implying phrasing beyond what the record supports.
No gore, grief, or panic as the hook.
No clickbait questions the video doesn't actually answer.
No title that just rephrases the thumbnail text — make them complement.
