Left in the Edit

Redaction / Reveal System

A visual language for cuts, omissions, and reveal moments

Version 1.0 · May 2026
Source: Brand Review & Action Plan
01

Why this exists

The visual idea behind the channel name

The name Left in the Edit is about what gets cut, omitted, or hidden. The brand needs a visual device that says the same thing: something was removed, something is being recovered, something is being revealed. That's what this system is for.

Six devices. All work together. Amber is reserved for the reveal point — never the redaction itself. That separation is what makes the system readable: charcoal hides, amber surfaces.

02

The six devices

Each one shows a different kind of "left in the edit"
01 Amber Reveal Line

A thin amber line that slides across text or an image to reveal the missing detail underneath. The hero move — use it once per video.

Officer statement · page 4
"I never drew my weapon during the stop."
Bodycam contradicts at 04:12
02 Redaction Bar

A solid charcoal/black bar covering a word, timestamp, face, or line of text. Treats the redaction as a real object on the page, not a graphic flourish.

Internal report · redacted
Witness █████████ stated the suspect "appeared ██████████" before officers arrived.
Released under FOIA 02-2026
03 Cut Marker

A small amber vertical slash or bracket showing exactly where the official edit cut. Use it when calling attention to where the recording stopped, the report ended, or the page broke.

Bodycam timeline · 04:11 → 04:13
"Sir, I need you to step out of the]
Camera stops · 2-second gap
04 Before / After Frame

A split between the official narrative and what the footage actually shows. The structural workhorse — use it whenever the case turns on a contradiction.

Official report
"Suspect was non-compliant from initial contact."
Footage
Suspect complies with commands for 14 seconds before contact.
05 Timestamp Reveal

A specific moment as the hook: 04:12, 11 minutes, 400 days. Single number, oversized, amber. Works as a thumbnail device and as an act break.

04:12
The moment the camera cut
06 Document Highlight

An amber underline or box around the exact phrase from a report, court filing, or court record. The "show, don't tell" device — let the source speak.

Incident Report · 26-04827
At approximately 19:42 hrs the reporting officer arrived on scene and observed the subject already in handcuffs prior to the arrival of unit 4-Tom-12. Subject was transported without further incident.
03

Usage Rules

Used wrong, the system turns the channel into a tabloid

The system only works if every redaction and every reveal corresponds to something real in the case file. The moment we start redacting random words because it looks dramatic, the credibility goes.

Do
Use redaction and reveal graphics to show missing context.
Reveal one key detail at a time.
Reserve amber for the reveal point — never the redaction.
Pair every reveal with a source label or timestamp where possible.
Use it for contradictions, omissions, timeline gaps, and official-report conflicts.
Match the device to what was actually cut: bar for redaction, line for hidden text, cut marker for stopped recording, before/after for narrative conflict.
Don't
Don't redact random words just to look mysterious.
Don't imply secret information where there is none.
Don't use amber on the redaction — it should hide, not glow.
Don't use more than one reveal device per moment.
Don't manufacture a "gap" the record doesn't actually contain.
Don't reveal something the viewer would also need to see in full context — that goes in the body of the video, not as a graphic device.
