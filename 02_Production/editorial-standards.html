Editorial Standards

Evidence before narrative · The rules every claim must pass

Version 1.0 · May 2026
Source: Brand Review & Action Plan
01

Principle

Evidence-led, not conclusion-led

The channel is evidence-led, not conclusion-led. Every claim in a script, thumbnail, title, or description must be supported by footage, court records, police statements, official documents, or clearly attributed reporting. If a claim cannot be sourced, it gets rewritten or removed — not softened.

These standards exist so the channel stays trustworthy as it grows. Once trust is lost it does not come back. Bake the rules into the pipeline and you stop having to make the same judgement call every week.

02

The Rules

9 rules · enforced at script and review
01
Every claim must be supported by footage, court records, police statements, official documents, or clearly attributed reporting.
02
Do not imply guilt beyond what the footage or records support.
03
Do not use gore, grief, or distress as the hook.
04
Do not include graphic moments unless they materially change the viewer's understanding.
05
Use careful attribution: allegedly, police say, court records state, the footage appears to show, according to the report.
06
Correct errors visibly. A pinned comment, a card, or an on-screen note — not a quiet edit.
07
Do not frame real people as characters for entertainment.
08
Do not overstate certainty where the record is unclear.
09
If a claim cannot be sourced, rewrite it or remove it. There is no third option.
03

Attribution Language

The phrases that protect the channel and the viewer

Attribution is what turns a serious claim into a defensible one. The exact wording matters. These are the phrases to use, and the framings to avoid.

Use
allegedly for unresolved or contested claims
police say when citing an officer's account
court records state for documented filings
the footage appears to show when visual evidence is ambiguous
according to the report for any specific report cited
the witness told investigators for statements in the file
Avoid
Stating contested events as fact
Calling someone a murderer, abuser, or criminal before a conviction
Implying motive that isn't in the record
Dramatising what someone was "thinking" or "feeling"
Using obviously, clearly, or without a doubt for inference
Mixing reporter speculation with documented fact
04

Pre-publish Test

Four questions every script must pass before it ships

Run the script through these four questions before it leaves Review. If any answer is "no" — or even "maybe" — fix it.

1
Is this supported by a source?
2
Is this phrased with the right level of certainty?
3
Would this still feel fair if the person involved watched it?
4
Does this moment inform the case, or is it only here because it is intense?
The line
If the answer to question 4 is "it's only here because it's intense" — cut it. Every time. Intensity that doesn't inform the case is exactly the kind of content that erodes trust.
