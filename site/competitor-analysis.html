02 · EWU dissected · your north star

EWU is the player to study. Goal isn't to copy · it's to take what scales for a one-person operation and ignore what only works at media-company scale.

The Narrator

Deep, slightly theatrical, broadcast-trained

The voice is a huge part of the brand · it signals "documentary, take it seriously."

WHAT YOU CAN DOSlow your pace. Drop your register slightly. Leave more space between sentences than feels natural. Time the pauses in a few EWU intros · they're longer than you'd expect.
The Framing

"A news agency committed to factual information"

EWU positions itself as journalism, not entertainment. That frames everything else.

WHAT YOU CAN DODraft a similar positioning line for your About page. Something like: "Left in the Edit is independent documentary journalism · primary footage, primary sources, the full record."
The Structure

30·60 min · cold open · context · walkthrough · aftermath

EWU videos follow a consistent shape: cold open (no narration) ? channel intro ? context (2·3 min) ? footage walkthrough ? aftermath ? outro tease.

WHAT YOU CAN DOYour 20-min target is shorter, which is fine. Use the cold open. Don't make viewers wait three minutes for the hook.
The Records Pipeline

FOIA requests at scale · sometimes via lawsuit

EWU files requests at scale and reportedly takes agencies to court when denied. That's the engine behind their "never-before-seen" claim.

WHAT YOU CAN DOBuild a request log (Google Sheet: date, agency, case, status, response). Over time this becomes a moat most creators won't bother building.
The Thumbnails

Mugshot or bodycam still + bold sans + coloured stripe

Faces large, two to three words max, high contrast. Deliberately local-news aesthetic.

WHAT YOU CAN DOStudy 30·50 of their thumbnails before designing yours. Genre legibility matters more than originality at thumbnail size.
The Blind Spot

Frequently described as cop-favourable

EWU rarely uses cases that make officers look bad. That's the daylight: include the unflattering footage, name what was cut.

WHAT YOU CAN DOThe differentiator is completeness, not angle. Stay calm and factual. The footage does the work. Don't editorialise.

04 · Where your daylight actually is

Three real positions you can hold. Pick the one that fits and commit.

The completeness positioning

"Left in the Edit" + your editorial standards + your willingness to include the awkward footage = a real position. EWU is fast and prolific but selective. You're slow and complete. Tell that story from video one.

Connected two-part cases

Most channels do one-offs. The Angie Frost arc is structurally rare · Part 1 ends with a teaser, Part 2 pays it off. If you can find more cases with this structure, it becomes your signature format.

Indie credibility

One-person operation with primary-source standards. That contrast · small operator, institutional rigour · is itself a story. Viewers who care about credibility will subscribe just for the editorial approach.

05 · What none of them do well

Gaps in the market you can own.

Most channels don't show their work. They cite "police records" without showing them. You can. Put the affidavit on screen. Highlight the line you're quoting.
No one does case follow-ups. Cases evolve · appeals, new charges, civil suits. There's almost no one doing follow-up content in this lane. The records-request muscle gives you material others won't have.
Community presence is weak across the board. EWU skews Facebook (older audience). JCS has none. There's room for a creator who engages on Reddit, replies in comments, and treats viewers as informed adults.
Few channels handle uncertainty well. Almost all of them present circumstantial evidence as fact. Strong evidence presented as unconfirmed until the affidavit lands is the right model, and rare.
06 · Concrete next moves

Seven actions. In order.

Watch 5 EWU videos with a notebook. Time the cold open. Time the first context beat. Note when the first evidence appears on screen. Note when narration changes pace. Reverse-engineer the rhythm, not the content.
Watch 2 JCS videos for the analytical-pause technique. When does JCS speed up footage? When does it pause? Find the rhythm.
Pick your channel positioning sentence. One line, on the about page, that filters every future decision. Draft three options, sit with them for a week, pick one.
Build a thumbnail style sheet · colour, font, face position, text length. Produce three sample thumbnails before your first upload.
Decide on-camera vs voice-only. Now, not later. Reshooting or rebranding is expensive.
Build a records-request log. Google Sheet: date sent, agency, case, request type, response date, status, file location.
Watch your competitors' worst videos too. Their lowest-view recent uploads tell you what doesn't work for the audience even when the channel is established.
07 · Honesty check

Four uncomfortable truths to budget for

  • EWU's scale isn't realistic for a part-time solo creator. Don't measure yourself against their cadence. JCS's ~19 videos over 6+ years is a more realistic ceiling for thoroughness-first creators.
  • The narrator voice matters more than people admit. EWU's narrator is a huge part of why the channel works. Your voice will need development time. Weekly test reads at home · even before you record Case 001 properly · are worth doing.
  • The records-request advantage compounds slowly. You won't have a moat for a year. Plan accordingly.
  • The space is crowded. Your differentiation has to be visible from the thumbnail and first 30 seconds. "We're more thorough" doesn't read on a thumbnail. "We show what others edited out" with a specific moment teased · does.
